A step-by-step explainer

Einstein Notation

By the end of this page you will be able to read, write, contract, and differentiate any tensor expression — and see why every identity in the Matrix Cookbook is a one-liner.

The whole idea, in one sentence

If the same index letter shows up twice in a single term, it means "sum over that letter." That's the whole convention. Everything else — contractions, traces, even derivatives — is just asking which letters are shared and which are free.

Step 1

Repeated index = sum

Consider the dot product of two vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The "normal" way to write it:

\[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i \, b_i \]

Einstein's observation: the \(\sum_{i}\) is redundant. You can see that \(i\) appears twice — once in \(a_i\) and once in \(b_i\). The only sensible thing to do with a repeated index is sum over it. So just drop the \(\sum\) sign and write:

\[ a_i \, b_i \quad \text{(means the same thing)} \]

That's the Einstein summation convention: an index that appears exactly twice in a term is implicitly summed over. No \(\sum\) needed. The widget below lets you pick the dimension and two vectors, and watch the summation unfold term by term.

The formal version

Convention. In any monomial expression, if an index variable appears exactly twice, a summation over the full range of that index is implied. Such an index is called a dummy index. An index appearing exactly once is a free index; the expression holds for each value of the free index.

The convention is well-defined only when each dummy index appears exactly twice. Three or more repetitions are ambiguous and not permitted.

Step 2

Free vs. dummy — what determines the shape

Once you drop the \(\sum\), every expression is just a string of indexed symbols. The indices that appear once are free — they label the output's axes. The indices that appear twice are dummy — they get summed away and disappear from the result.

Quick rule of thumb: count the free indices → that tells you the tensor rank of the result.

\[ \underbrace{a_i b_i}_{\text{0 free → scalar}} \qquad \underbrace{A_{ij} b_j}_{\text{1 free }(i)\text{ → vector}} \qquad \underbrace{A_{ik} B_{kj}}_{\text{2 free }(i,j)\text{ → matrix}} \]

The widget lets you type any index expression and see the classification instantly.

Try: a_i b_i (scalar), A_ij x_j (vector), A_ik B_kj (matrix), C_ijk x_j y_k (vector).

Step 3

Every matrix operation is an index pattern

Here's where it gets satisfying. Every operation from linear algebra is just a particular arrangement of shared and free indices. You don't need to memorize "matrix multiplication is rows times columns" — you just need to see which indices are shared:

Each operation is fully determined by which indices are free and which are summed.

Complete table of common operations

Here's the full reference. Every one of these is obtained by just deciding which letters to share:

\(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_i b_i\) — dot product
\((\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x})_i = A_{ij} x_j\) — matrix-vector product
\((\mathbf{AB})_{ij} = A_{ik} B_{kj}\) — matrix-matrix product
\(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = A_{ii}\) — trace
\((\mathbf{a} \otimes \mathbf{b})_{ij} = a_i b_j\) — outer product (no repeated index!)
\((\mathbf{A}^T)_{ij} = A_{ji}\) — transpose (just swap the subscripts)
\(\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = x_i A_{ij} x_j\) — quadratic form

Step 4

Contraction = glue two indices, then sum

Contraction sounds intimidating but it's embarrassingly simple: take two free indices and declare them to be the same letter. That letter now appears twice, so it gets summed away. The result has fewer indices (lower rank) than you started with.

Matrix trace is the canonical example: \(A_{ij}\) is a matrix (rank 2, two free indices). Set \(j = i\) → \(A_{ii}\) has no free indices left → scalar. That scalar is \(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A})\).

Matrix multiplication is a contraction too: start with \(A_{ik}\) and \(B_{\ell j}\), set \(\ell = k\) → \(A_{ik} B_{kj}\), the shared \(k\) gets summed away, leaving a matrix indexed by \((i,j)\).

Step 5

Derivatives: just the product rule on indices

Here's the payoff. In Einstein notation, taking a derivative with respect to a matrix \(X_{mn}\) is just:

\[ \frac{\partial\, X_{ij}}{\partial\, X_{mn}} = \delta_{im}\,\delta_{jn} \]

where \(\delta_{im}\) is the Kronecker delta (1 if \(i = m\), 0 otherwise). Read it as: "the \((i,j)\) entry of \(X\) only responds to \(X_{mn}\) when \(i=m\) and \(j=n\)." That's obvious — each entry is independent of the others. The \(\delta\)'s just say that precisely.

Now, when you differentiate a product, you use the normal product rule:

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial X_{mn}}(A_{ik} X_{kj}) = A_{ik}\,\delta_{km}\,\delta_{jn} = A_{im}\,\delta_{jn} \]

The \(\delta\)'s simply replace the contracted indices: \(k \to m\) and \(j \to n\). This is the whole trick. The widget shows this substitution happening live for any expression you build.

Watch the Kronecker deltas fire and replace indices. This is the entire mechanism behind every Matrix Cookbook identity.

The Kronecker delta and index substitution

The key identity is \(\delta_{im} \, A_{ij\ldots} = A_{mj\ldots}\). Contracting a Kronecker delta with any tensor simply replaces the shared index. This is the analogue of "sifting" — the delta picks out the one term in the sum where the indices match.

Because \(\frac{\partial X_{ij}}{\partial X_{mn}} = \delta_{im}\delta_{jn}\), differentiating any expression that is linear in \(X\) amounts to: (1) find where \(X\) sits, (2) replace its indices with the derivative indices via deltas, (3) contract the deltas away. Mechanical and error-free.

Why this always works

Any scalar-valued function of a matrix \(X\) that is built from traces of products can be written as a fully contracted index expression. Differentiating with respect to \(X_{mn}\) inserts \(\delta_{im}\delta_{jn}\) at the position of each \(X_{ij}\). Since the expression was fully contracted (a scalar), after the deltas fire, exactly two free indices \((m, n)\) remain — giving a matrix, which is the gradient \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial X_{mn}}\). Re-reading the surviving index pattern as a matrix operation gives the Matrix Cookbook result.

This procedure is completely mechanical. No tricks, no cleverness — just the product rule and delta substitution. It works for every identity in the Cookbook that involves traces of products.

Step 7

Upper vs. lower — vectors and covectors

So far we've been putting every index downstairs: \(a_i\), \(A_{ij}\). That works in Euclidean space with an orthonormal basis. But the full Einstein convention distinguishes two kinds of index — and the distinction is not formalism for its own sake. Vectors and covectors are geometrically different objects, and the notation tracks that difference so you can't accidentally mix them up.

\[ v^i \;\;\text{(upper index — a \textbf{vector})} \qquad\qquad w_i \;\;\text{(lower index — a \textbf{covector})} \]

The summation rule, properly stated: you only sum when the same letter appears once upper and once lower. Two uppers or two lowers can't be contracted without a metric to convert one of them first.

A vector \(v^i\) (upper index) is a thing with direction and magnitude — a velocity, a displacement, a parameter update. A covector \(w_i\) (lower index) is a linear functional that eats vectors and produces numbers — a gradient, a price list, a measurement. The contraction \(w_i v^i\) is the covector evaluated on the vector: a scalar.

7a  ·  Why they transform in opposite directions

The price–quantity analogy makes the transformation law unforgettable. Quantities of goods are a vector \(q^i\) (upper index): "3 apples, 5 bananas." Prices per unit are a covector \(p_i\) (lower index): "$1.50/apple, $0.75/banana." The total cost is the contraction:

\[ \text{cost} = p_i \, q^i = (1.50)(3) + (0.75)(5) = \$8.25 \]

Now switch units from "apples" to "dozens of apples." The quantity number shrinks by 12× (you bought 0.25 dozen, not 3). The price grows by 12× ($18/dozen instead of $1.50/each). They scale in opposite directions — and the total cost is unchanged. That's what contravariant (upper, scales against the basis change) and covariant (lower, scales with the basis change) mean. The notation makes this mistake-proof: \(p_i + q^i\) (dollars-per-apple + number-of-apples) is syntactically illegal.

7b  ·  The metric tensor: raising and lowering indices

The metric tensor \(g_{ij}\) is the bridge between vectors and covectors. Its index positions tell you exactly what it does:

\(g_{ij}\) has two lower indices because it eats two vectors (upper index objects) and spits out a scalar — the inner product \(g_{ij}\,v^i w^j = \langle v, w \rangle\). Feed it just one vector to lower its index: \(v_i = g_{ij}\,v^j\). This converts a vector (direction) into a covector (measurement).

\(g^{ij}\) has two upper indices — it is the inverse of \(g_{ij}\). It eats two covectors or raises a single covector's index: \(v^i = g^{ij}\,v_j\). This converts a measurement back into a direction.

Concrete example: the sphere. On a sphere of radius \(R\), using coordinates \((\theta, \phi)\) = (latitude, longitude), the metric is:

\[ g_{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} R^2 & 0 \\ 0 & R^2\cos^2\!\theta \end{pmatrix} \qquad g^{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/R^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/(R^2\cos^2\!\theta) \end{pmatrix} \]

Why \(\cos^2\!\theta\) in the \(\phi\phi\)-entry? Because longitude lines converge at the poles. One radian of longitude covers \(R\) meters at the equator but nearly zero meters near a pole. The metric encodes this: "how much actual distance does one coordinate step cost?"

Lowering: suppose you walk at \(v^i = (1, 1)\) — one radian/sec in each coordinate direction. The lowered covector is \(v_i = g_{ij} v^j = (R^2,\; R^2 \cos^2\!\theta)\). At the equator both components are \(R^2\) — the two directions cost the same. Near a pole the \(\phi\)-component shrinks to almost nothing, because one radian of longitude barely moves you.

Raising: suppose the temperature gradient covector is \(dT_i = (5, 5)\) — the temperature changes at 5 degrees per radian in both coordinates. The raised gradient is \((\nabla T)^i = g^{ij}\,dT_j = (5/R^2, \; 5/(R^2\cos^2\!\theta))\). Near a pole the \(\phi\)-component gets amplified: the longitude circles are so small that even a modest temperature change per radian means a steep change per meter. The steepest-ascent direction tilts toward \(\phi\), even though the measurement was equal in both coordinates.

Left: lowering — the vector \(v^i\) (gold bars) becomes the covector \(v_i\) (blue bars) via \(g_{ij}\). Right: raising — a fixed covector \(dT_i = (5,5)\) (blue) becomes the gradient vector (green) via \(g^{ij}\). Move latitude toward a pole and watch the \(\phi\)-components diverge.

7c  ·  What is a dual space, really?

Before we go to manifolds, we need one definition. You'll see the phrase "cotangent space is the dual of the tangent space" everywhere. What does "dual" actually mean?

Start with any vector space \(V\) — say, \(\mathbb{R}^2\). Its dual space \(V^*\) is defined as:

\[ V^* = \{\;\text{all linear functions } \varphi : V \to \mathbb{R}\;\} \]

That's it. \(V^*\) is the collection of every possible linear machine that eats a vector and spits out a single number. Each such machine is called a linear functional, or equivalently, a covector.

Why is this a space? Because you can add two linear functionals (apply both, add the results) and scale them, and the result is again a linear functional. So \(V^*\) is itself a vector space — with the same dimension as \(V\).

The key insight: if \(V\) is \(n\)-dimensional, each linear functional \(\varphi\) is completely determined by \(n\) numbers — the values it assigns to the \(n\) basis vectors: \(\varphi_i = \varphi(\mathbf{e}_i)\). Those numbers are the components of the covector, and they carry a lower index.

So in coordinates, a covector looks like a list of \(n\) numbers — just like a vector. But its job is different: a vector is a thing, a covector measures things. The action of a covector \(\varphi\) on a vector \(v\) is:

\[ \varphi(v) = \varphi_i \, v^i \quad \text{(one lower, one upper — Einstein summation!)} \]

This is why the summation convention demands one upper and one lower: it's encoding the fact that a covector is eating a vector. The result is a number — a scalar — which is an object with zero indices, exactly as the convention produces.

A basis for \(V^*\): given a basis \(\{\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n\}\) for \(V\), there is a natural dual basis \(\{\mathbf{e}^1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}^n\}\) for \(V^*\), defined by the rule: \(\mathbf{e}^i(\mathbf{e}_j) = \delta^i_j\). Each dual basis vector is the linear functional that returns 1 on its "own" basis vector and 0 on all others. It's the "detector" for the \(i\)-th component.

Left: the vector space \(V\) with the vector (gold arrow) and the covector's level lines (blue). Right: the dual space \(V^*\) with the covector as a point. Both are 2D spaces, but they contain different kinds of objects. The covector's level lines are the sets where \(\varphi(v) = \text{const}\) — and the value of the contraction is which level line the arrow tip lands on.

Why \(V\) and \(V^*\) are not "the same space"

In finite dimensions, \(V\) and \(V^*\) have the same dimension and are therefore isomorphic as vector spaces. But there is no canonical (basis-independent) isomorphism between them without extra structure. You need a bilinear form — an inner product, a metric — to identify one with the other.

In \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the standard dot product, the canonical identification \(v^i \leftrightarrow v_i\) via \(v_i = \delta_{ij} v^j\) is so natural that most people never realize \(V\) and \(V^*\) are different. But the moment you change the inner product (a non-trivial metric \(g_{ij}\)), the identification changes, and the distinction becomes visible. This is exactly the story of sections 7b–7d.

7d  ·  Temperature on Earth: vectors, covectors, and the spaces they live in

Let's put it all together with a concrete example. The Earth's surface is a 2-sphere \(S^2\) — a curved 2-dimensional manifold. (Note: the sphere \(S^2\) is the manifold we walk on; SO(3) is the group of rotations that acts on it. They're different objects — more on this in 7e.)

At any point \(p\) on the sphere, the tangent space \(T_p S^2\) is the flat plane that just touches the surface — all the directions an ant could walk. Vectors live here. A vector \(v^i \in T_p S^2\) is a velocity: "I'm walking northeast at 3 km/h."

Now suppose there's a temperature function \(T\) defined everywhere on the sphere — warm at the equator, cold at the poles. The differential \(dT\) at a point is a covector: it's the linear functional that takes a velocity \(v\) and tells you how fast the temperature changes if you walk in that direction:

\[ dT(v) = \frac{\partial T}{\partial x^i}\,v^i = (\nabla_i T)\,v^i \quad \in \mathbb{R} \]

This is a contraction — one lower index (\(\nabla_i T\)) meets one upper index (\(v^i\)) — producing a scalar: the rate of temperature change in °C per hour.

The covector \(dT\) does not point anywhere. It's not an arrow on the tangent plane — it's a linear functional that lives in the cotangent space \(T_p^* S^2\) (the dual of the tangent space). Geometrically, it looks like the temperature contour lines on the tangent plane — the level sets of \(T\). Walking perpendicular to the contours changes the temperature fastest; walking parallel to them doesn't change it at all.

To get a direction of steepest ascent — an actual arrow you can follow — you need to raise the index with the metric: \((\nabla T)^i = g^{ij}\,\nabla_j T\). On the sphere with the standard metric, this is just the ordinary gradient vector. But the covector \(dT\) (the measurement machine) exists before you choose a metric; the gradient vector (the arrow) requires one.

\[ \underbrace{v^i \in T_p S^2}_{\text{velocity — "I walk northeast"}} \qquad \underbrace{dT_i \in T_p^* S^2}_{\text{temperature gradient — "how fast does T change?"}} \qquad \underbrace{dT_i\,v^i \in \mathbb{R}}_{\text{contraction — "+2.3 °C/hr"}} \]

The sphere is color-coded by temperature (warm red at equator, cold blue at poles). The tangent plane at your location shows: the velocity vector (gold arrow), the temperature contour lines (blue — the covector \(dT\)), and the gradient arrow (green — \(dT\) with its index raised). Walk south from the Arctic and \(dT(v)\) is positive (warming up); walk along a contour line and it's zero.

7e  ·  Concrete example: SO(3) and angular velocity

SO(3) is the group of all 3D rotation matrices — matrices \(R\) satisfying \(R^T R = I\) and \(\det R = 1\). It's a 3-dimensional curved manifold living inside the 9-dimensional space of all 3×3 matrices. You can't parametrize it with 3 numbers without singularities (that's why Euler angles have gimbal lock).

Tangent vectors at the identity: if you take a curve of rotations \(R(t)\) with \(R(0) = I\), then \(\dot{R}(0) = \Omega\) must be a skew-symmetric matrix — that is, \(\Omega^T = -\Omega\). This is because differentiating \(R^T R = I\) at \(t = 0\) gives \(\Omega^T + \Omega = 0\). The space of 3×3 skew-symmetric matrices is 3-dimensional (it's the Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\)), spanned by:

\[ L_1 = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&-1\\0&1&0\end{pmatrix}\quad L_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\\0&0&0\\-1&0&0\end{pmatrix}\quad L_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix} \]

Any tangent vector is \(\Omega = \omega^1 L_1 + \omega^2 L_2 + \omega^3 L_3\). The three numbers \((\omega^1, \omega^2, \omega^3)\) are the angular velocity — and the upper index tells you it's a vector in \(T_I\,\text{SO}(3)\).

Covectors at the identity are things that eat angular velocities and output numbers. The canonical example is angular momentum in mechanics, or the gradient of a cost function \(f(R)\) on rotations. If you're optimizing over rotations (say, aligning a 3D scan), the gradient \(\partial f / \partial \omega_i\) is a covector — it tells you "how much does the cost change per unit of rotation about each axis." To get an actual direction to rotate (a vector), you need to raise the index with a metric.

The natural metric on SO(3) is the Killing form: \(\langle \Omega_1, \Omega_2 \rangle = -\tfrac{1}{2}\,\text{tr}(\Omega_1 \Omega_2)\). In the \((\omega^1, \omega^2, \omega^3)\) coordinates, this is just the identity matrix — so at the identity, raising and lowering are trivial. But at other points on SO(3), the metric in a given coordinate chart is non-trivial, and the distinction matters.

The angular velocity \(\omega^i\) (upper index — a tangent vector) generates a rotation via the exponential map \(R = e^{\Omega}\). The 3D axes on the left show the rotated frame. The matrices on the right show the skew-symmetric tangent vector \(\Omega\) and the resulting rotation \(R\).

The Lie algebra as tangent space — the general pattern

SO(3) is a Lie group, and its tangent space at the identity is the Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\). This pattern generalizes: every Lie group \(G\) has a Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{g} = T_e G\), and the exponential map \(\exp: \mathfrak{g} \to G\) sends tangent vectors to group elements.

In Einstein notation, the Killing form metric on \(\mathfrak{so}(3)\) is \(g_{ij} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\text{tr}(L_i L_j) = \delta_{ij}\), so in the \((\omega^1, \omega^2, \omega^3)\) basis, raising and lowering are trivial at the identity. But if you parametrize rotations with Euler angles \((\phi, \theta, \psi)\), the metric in those coordinates is non-diagonal and depends on the angles — lowering an index genuinely changes the components, and that's exactly where gimbal lock and other coordinate pathologies come from.

Why this matters for ML: the natural gradient

The loss gradient \(\nabla_i \mathcal{L}\) is naturally a covector (lower index) — it measures how the loss changes per unit of each parameter, like a price list. But to update parameters, you need a vector (upper index) — a direction to step in.

In vanilla SGD, you use \(g^{ij} = \delta^{ij}\), so raising the index is invisible: \(\Delta\theta^i = -\eta\,\nabla_i \mathcal{L}\). But parameter space is not Euclidean — reparametrizing the model changes what "one unit" means along each axis. The Fisher information matrix \(F_{ij}\) is the natural Riemannian metric on statistical models. The natural gradient raises the index properly:

\[\Delta\theta^i = -\eta\,F^{ij}\,\nabla_j \mathcal{L}\]

Adam, AdaGrad, and K-FAC are all approximations of this — they approximate \(F^{ij}\) with cheaper-to-compute matrices. The entire field of "adaptive learning rates" is secretly about finding a better metric to raise the gradient's index.

Putting it together

Einstein notation is the rule that a repeated index means sum. That's it. Once you adopt this rule:

Every matrix operation becomes a pattern of shared index letters. Contraction means declaring two indices equal (and summing them away). The derivative of \(X_{ij}\) with respect to \(X_{mn}\) is \(\delta_{im}\delta_{jn}\), and differentiating any expression is just the product rule followed by letting the deltas fire — mechanically replacing indices. The Matrix Cookbook's 70 pages of identities are not 70 separate facts. They are one fact applied over and over.

And when you're ready to go deeper: the full convention distinguishes upper indices (vectors, velocities, contravariant) from lower indices (covectors, gradients, covariant). A metric tensor converts between the two. In Euclidean space with an orthonormal basis the distinction is invisible — which is why introductory linear algebra never mentions it. But on curved manifolds, in non-orthogonal coordinates, and in information geometry, the distinction is real — and the notation is already set up for it.

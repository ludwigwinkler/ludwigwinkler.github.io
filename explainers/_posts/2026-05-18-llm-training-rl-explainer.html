Training LLMs: From Pretraining to the Reinforcement Learning Revolution

A deep dive into how modern language models are built — from raw text ingestion through supervised fine-tuning to the RL breakthroughs that produced reasoning models like o1, R1, and Claude. With interactive visualizations of every key idea.

The whole idea, in one sentence

LLM training is a three-act play: first the model learns the statistical structure of language (pretraining), then it learns to follow instructions (SFT), and finally reinforcement learning reshapes its behavior toward goals that pure imitation can never capture — helpfulness, reasoning depth, safety, and alignment with human judgment.

Step 1

The Three Stages of LLM Training

Every frontier LLM goes through roughly the same three-stage pipeline. Stage 1: Pretraining consumes trillions of tokens and teaches the model the statistical structure of language — syntax, facts, reasoning patterns — by predicting the next token. The loss is simple:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{PT}} = -\sum_{t} \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{

This produces a powerful but undirected model — it can continue any text, but it doesn't know it's supposed to be helpful. Stage 2: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) takes curated (instruction, response) pairs and trains the model to follow instructions. It's straightforward cross-entropy on human-written demonstrations.

But SFT has a ceiling: the model can only be as good as the demonstrations. It imitates, it doesn't optimize. Stage 3: Reinforcement Learning breaks through that ceiling. Instead of copying human outputs, the model generates its own responses and receives a reward signal telling it how good they are. It then adjusts its policy to produce more of the high-reward behavior — including behavior no human ever wrote down.

The formal framing

Pretraining maximizes \(\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}}[\log p_\theta(x)]\) over a corpus \(\mathcal{D}\). SFT maximizes \(\mathbb{E}_{(q,a) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\text{sft}}}[\log p_\theta(a \mid q)]\) over curated demonstrations. RL maximizes \(\mathbb{E}_{q \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid q)}[R(q, y)] - \beta \, \text{KL}[\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}}]\), where \(R\) is a reward function and the KL term prevents the policy from drifting too far from the SFT checkpoint.

Step 2

The RL Environment for Language: Mapping the MDP

Before we can apply RL, we need to define the environment — the formal setup that tells us what the "agent" is, what "actions" it can take, and where the "reward" comes from. In classic RL (think Atari or robotics), the environment is a game or a physics simulator. For LLMs, the environment is the act of generating text itself.

Here's how the standard Markov Decision Process (MDP) maps onto language generation:

State \(s_t\) = the prompt concatenated with all tokens generated so far: \(s_t = [x, y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_{t-1}]\). Each new token extends the state. The initial state \(s_0 = x\) is just the prompt.

Action \(a_t\) = the next token \(y_t\) chosen from the vocabulary \(\mathcal{V}\). The action space is enormous — typically 32K–128K discrete choices at every step. This is orders of magnitude larger than Atari (18 actions) or Go (361 positions).

Policy \(\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)\) = the LLM itself. Its softmax output over the vocabulary is the policy — the probability distribution over next tokens given the current state.

Transition = deterministic. Taking action \(a_t = y_t\) from state \(s_t\) always produces state \(s_{t+1} = [s_t, y_t]\). There's no stochastic environment dynamics — the only randomness comes from the policy's own sampling.

Reward \(r\) = typically sparse, delivered only at the end of the episode (when the model finishes generating). The reward model or verifier scores the complete response. This creates a severe credit assignment problem: which of the 500 tokens in the response was responsible for the high (or low) reward? This is fundamentally harder than Atari, where reward comes every few frames.

Episode = one complete generation. The model starts with prompt \(x\), generates tokens until it produces an end-of-sequence token (or hits a length limit), and receives a reward for the full response.

Constructing the prompt distribution is itself a design choice — the "environment design" of LLM RL. DeepSeek R1 used tens of thousands of math and code problems. Anthropic samples from real user conversations. The prompt distribution shapes what the model learns to be good at, just like choosing training levels in a game.

The LLM is the policy; text generation is the episode; the reward comes at the end.

Toggle between the MDP abstraction and a concrete episode walkthrough.

Why sparse rewards make credit assignment hard

In the bandit formulation (reward at end only), the policy gradient for a complete response \(y = (y_1, \ldots, y_T)\) is: \[ \nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}\left[R(x, y) \sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_t | s_t)\right] \] A single scalar \(R\) multiplies the gradient contribution of every token equally. Token 3 (which set up the right approach) and token 487 (which was just filler) get the same credit. This is why advantage estimation and process reward models matter — they try to distribute credit more precisely.

Step 3

Reward Models: Teaching a Critic to Score

The reward model (RM) is the compass that tells RL which direction is "better". Get it right, and your model improves. Get it wrong, and RL will ruthlessly exploit every flaw. Reward model design is arguably the single most consequential engineering decision in the RLHF pipeline.

Architecture. An RM is typically the same transformer architecture as the LLM itself (often initialized from the SFT checkpoint), but with the language-modeling head replaced by a single linear layer that outputs a scalar score: \(r_\phi(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}\). The model reads the full (prompt, response) pair and produces one number representing quality.

Training data. Human annotators are shown a prompt and two (or more) responses. They rank them by preference. These pairwise comparisons form the training set. InstructGPT used ~33K comparison pairs. Modern RMs use hundreds of thousands.

Training objective. The Bradley-Terry model assumes human preferences follow a logistic model over reward differences:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l)}\left[\log \sigma\bigl(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\bigr)\right] \]

This is just binary cross-entropy: push the preferred response's score higher than the rejected one's. The gap between scores approximates how much better one response is.

Outcome vs. Process Reward Models. An outcome reward model (ORM) scores the final response as a whole. A process reward model (PRM) scores each intermediate reasoning step. PRMs were explored heavily in 2023–2024 (OpenAI's "Let's Verify Step by Step"), but DeepSeek R1 found that PRMs didn't justify their overhead at scale — outcome rewards with GRPO were more efficient. This remains an active debate.

Failure modes — Goodhart's Law. "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The RM is a proxy for human judgment. When RL optimizes too aggressively against it, the policy finds responses that score highly according to the RM but look worse to actual humans. This is reward hacking (or reward overoptimization). Common hacks include: generating excessively long responses (RMs tend to prefer longer text), repeating confident-sounding phrases, or producing plausible-looking but incorrect reasoning chains. The KL penalty exists precisely to limit this.

More pairs and higher agreement both improve RM accuracy, but there's a ceiling — and beyond it, the RM's proxy nature means more data doesn't help.

Reward overoptimization — the scaling law

Gao et al. (2022) found that the relationship between RL optimization pressure and true reward follows: \[ R_{\text{true}}(d) = \alpha \sqrt{d} - \beta\, d \] where \(d = \text{KL}[\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}}]\). The first term (\(\sqrt{d}\)) captures genuine improvement — the RM is partially correct. The second term (\(-d\)) captures overoptimization — the further you push, the more you exploit RM errors. The optimum is at \(d^* = \frac{\alpha^2}{4\beta^2}\). Push past this point and true quality decreases even as RM score continues to rise. This is why the KL penalty coefficient \(\beta\) must be carefully tuned — it controls how far you travel along this curve.

Step 4

From REINFORCE to PPO: The Policy Gradient Engine

With the environment defined and the reward model trained, we need an algorithm to actually update the policy. The foundation is the policy gradient theorem — the idea that you can compute the gradient of expected reward with respect to the policy parameters, even though the reward is non-differentiable.

REINFORCE (Williams, 1992) is the simplest policy gradient algorithm. The gradient of expected reward is:

\[ \nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi_\theta}\!\left[R(y) \cdot \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x)\right] \]

Intuitively: sample a response, compute its reward, and if the reward is high, increase the probability of that response (push the log-prob gradient in the positive direction). If the reward is low, decrease it. The reward acts as a multiplier on the gradient.

The problem: REINFORCE has extremely high variance. One lucky sample with a high reward can send the gradient wildly off course. The fix is to subtract a baseline \(b\) — a running estimate of average reward — so the gradient becomes:

\[ \nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}\!\left[(R(y) - b) \cdot \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x)\right] \]

The quantity \(A(y) = R(y) - b\) is the advantage — how much better this response was compared to average. Positive advantage → reinforce. Negative → suppress.

PPO (Schulman, 2017) built on REINFORCE with two key innovations. First, it uses a learned value function \(V_\psi(s)\) (the "critic") for a much better baseline, computing advantage via Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE). Second, it uses a clipped objective that prevents catastrophically large policy updates — the "proximal" in PPO. Let \(\rho_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t|s_t)}\) be the probability ratio. PPO maximizes:

\[ L^{\text{CLIP}} = \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\min\!\left(\rho_t \hat{A}_t,\; \text{clip}(\rho_t, 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon)\,\hat{A}_t\right)\right] \]

If the policy changes too much (ratio far from 1), the clip kicks in and stops the update. This keeps training stable. PPO was the algorithm behind ChatGPT, Claude (early versions), and most 2022–2023 era RLHF systems.

The cost: PPO requires four models in memory simultaneously: (1) the policy \(\pi_\theta\), (2) the reference policy \(\pi_\text{ref}\) for KL, (3) the reward model \(r_\phi\), and (4) the value/critic model \(V_\psi\). For a 70B-parameter LLM, that's ~280B parameters of GPU memory. This engineering burden is what motivated the search for simpler alternatives.

Low β lets the policy chase reward — but a weak reward model causes reward hacking. High β keeps the policy safe but limits improvement. Find the balance.

GAE and the bias-variance tradeoff in advantage estimation

Generalized Advantage Estimation computes \(\hat{A}_t = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(\gamma\lambda)^l \delta_{t+l}\) where \(\delta_t = r_t + \gamma V(s_{t+1}) - V(s_t)\). The parameter \(\lambda \in [0,1]\) trades bias for variance: \(\lambda = 0\) gives a one-step TD estimate (low variance, high bias if \(V\) is wrong); \(\lambda = 1\) gives the full Monte Carlo return (unbiased but high variance). In LLM RLHF, rewards are sparse (end-of-episode only), so \(\delta_t = -V(s_t)\) for all intermediate steps and \(\delta_T = R - V(s_T)\) at the end. This makes \(V\) especially important — a bad value function means bad advantages, which means learning noise instead of signal.

Step 5

DPO: Cutting Out the Middleman (2023)

In May 2023, Rafailov et al. at Stanford dropped a bombshell: you don't actually need a separate reward model or PPO at all. The core insight of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is that the constrained reward-maximization problem in RLHF has a closed-form solution. If you substitute that solution back into the Bradley-Terry loss, you get a simple classification-style objective you can optimize directly on preference pairs:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)\right] \]

DPO eliminates the reward model, eliminates PPO, eliminates online generation — it's just supervised learning on preference pairs. This was a genuine step function: suddenly any lab could do alignment without the engineering complexity of PPO. Models like Zephyr, Tulu, and many open-source LLMs adopted DPO almost overnight.

The trade-off? DPO is an offline method — it only learns from a fixed dataset of preferences. It doesn't explore, it doesn't discover new behaviors. For simple alignment tasks (tone, helpfulness), this is often fine. For complex reasoning? PPO and its descendants still win, because exploration matters. A 2024 benchmark study confirmed that PPO outperformed DPO on challenging code-generation tasks, while DPO matched it on summarization and dialogue.

Watch how DPO reshapes log-probability ratios. High β means conservative updates; low β means aggressive preference enforcement (risk of overfitting).

The full DPO derivation — from RLHF objective to binary classifier

1. The RLHF objective. Standard RLHF seeks a policy \(\pi\) that maximizes expected reward while staying close to a reference policy \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\):

\[ \max_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi(\cdot|x)}\bigl[r(x, y)\bigr] - \beta \, \text{KL}\bigl[\pi(\cdot|x) \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(\cdot|x)\bigr] \]

2. Deriving the closed-form optimal policy. We need to find the distribution \(\pi^*(\cdot|x)\) that maximizes the objective in step 1. Expanding the KL divergence, our objective for a fixed prompt \(x\) becomes:

\[ \max_{\pi} \sum_{y} \pi(y|x)\, r(x,y) - \beta \sum_{y} \pi(y|x) \log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} \]

subject to the constraint that \(\pi(\cdot|x)\) is a valid probability distribution: \(\sum_y \pi(y|x) = 1\) and \(\pi(y|x) \geq 0\).

This is a constrained optimization over a probability simplex. We form the Lagrangian, introducing a multiplier \(\lambda\) for the normalization constraint:

\[ \mathcal{L}(\pi, \lambda) = \sum_{y} \pi(y|x)\, r(x,y) - \beta \sum_{y} \pi(y|x) \log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} + \lambda\!\left(1 - \sum_{y} \pi(y|x)\right) \]

Take the functional derivative with respect to \(\pi(y|x)\) for a specific \(y\), and set it to zero:

\[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \pi(y|x)} = r(x,y) - \beta\!\left(\log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} + 1\right) - \lambda = 0 \]

(The \(+1\) comes from differentiating \(\pi \log \pi\): \(\frac{d}{d\pi}[\pi \log \pi] = \log \pi + 1\).)

Solve for \(\pi(y|x)\). Rearranging the stationarity condition:

\[ \log \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} = \frac{1}{\beta}\bigl(r(x,y) - \lambda - \beta\bigr) \]

Exponentiate both sides:

\[ \frac{\pi(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} = \exp\!\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right) \cdot \underbrace{\exp\!\left(\frac{-\lambda - \beta}{\beta}\right)}_{\text{constant in } y} \]

Therefore:

\[ \pi(y|x) = \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \cdot \exp\!\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right) \cdot C(x) \]

where \(C(x) = \exp\!\bigl(\frac{-\lambda - \beta}{\beta}\bigr)\) absorbs everything that doesn't depend on \(y\).

Enforce normalization. Since \(\sum_y \pi(y|x) = 1\), we can solve for \(C(x)\):

\[ 1 = C(x) \sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad C(x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \]

where we define the partition function:

\[ Z(x) \triangleq \sum_{y} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right) \]

This gives us the optimal policy — a Gibbs (Boltzmann) distribution tilted from the reference by exponentiated reward:

\[ \boxed{\pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)}\, \pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)\, \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\beta}\, r(x, y)\right)} \]

Intuition: every response gets a "boost" proportional to \(\exp(r/\beta)\) relative to the reference policy. High-reward responses get exponentially up-weighted; low-reward responses get suppressed. The temperature \(\beta\) controls how aggressively: as \(\beta \to 0\), the policy collapses to the single best response; as \(\beta \to \infty\), the policy stays at \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\). The partition function \(Z(x)\) is intractable — it sums over all possible responses — but DPO will eliminate it in step 4.

3. Reparameterize reward. Take the log and solve for \(r\):

\[ r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)} + \underbrace{\beta \log Z(x)}_{\text{depends only on } x} \]

4. Substitute into Bradley-Terry. The reward model loss uses \(p(y_w \succ y_l) = \sigma(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l))\). When we compute the reward difference:

\[ r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} + \underbrace{\beta \log Z(x) - \beta \log Z(x)}_{= \; 0 \text{ (cancels!)}} - \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)} \]

The intractable partition function \(Z(x)\) cancels — this is the crux of DPO.

5. The DPO loss. Replace \(\pi^*\) with our trainable \(\pi_\theta\):

\[ \boxed{\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l)}\left[\log \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w|x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l|x)}\right)\right]} \]

This is binary cross-entropy. The sigmoid argument is the "implicit reward margin."

6. Gradient intuition.

\[ \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}} = -\beta\, \mathbb{E}\!\left[\underbrace{\sigma(\hat{r}_l - \hat{r}_w)}_{\text{how wrong the model is}}\Big[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_w|x) - \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_l|x)\Big]\right] \]

where \(\hat{r}_y = \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)}\). The weighting \(\sigma(\hat{r}_l - \hat{r}_w)\) is large when the model currently ranks the loser above the winner — a built-in hard-example mining.

Step 6

GRPO and DeepSeek R1: The Reasoning Revolution (Jan 2025)

January 2025 was a watershed. DeepSeek released R1 — an open-weight reasoning model that matched OpenAI's o1 on math, code, and STEM benchmarks — at a fraction of the cost. The secret was Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), and a radical training philosophy: skip SFT entirely, apply RL directly to the base model.

GRPO's key innovation is eliminating the critic (value) model that PPO requires. Recall from Step 4 that PPO needs a separate neural network — often as large as the policy itself — to estimate the "value" of each state. This doubles your memory and compute. GRPO replaces the critic with a beautifully simple idea:

For each prompt, sample a group of \(G\) responses. Score them all with the reward function. Compute the advantage of each response as its reward relative to the group mean:

\[ A_i = \frac{r_i - \text{mean}(\{r_j\})}{\text{std}(\{r_j\})} \]

No critic model. No value function estimation. The group itself serves as the baseline — a Monte Carlo estimate of the expected reward, which is exactly the variance-reducing baseline we discussed in REINFORCE. This cut training costs dramatically and, combined with rule-based verifiable rewards, enabled what DeepSeek called Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR).

The most stunning result: DeepSeek-R1-Zero, trained with pure RL from the base model (no SFT), spontaneously developed chain-of-thought reasoning, self-verification, and reflection — behaviors that were never explicitly taught. The RL environment (Step 2) incentivized them because they lead to correct answers, and the reward model (Step 3) — in this case a simple correctness checker — reinforced them.

GRPO vs PPO — memory and compute comparison

PPO's advantages are computed via GAE from a learned value function: 4 models in memory (policy, reference, reward model, critic). GRPO drops the critic entirely — 3 models max, and with RLVR (rule-based rewards) it's just 2: policy + reference.

The policy gradient under GRPO: \[ \nabla_\theta J \approx \frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^G A_i \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_i | x) \] with PPO-style clipping on probability ratios. DeepSeek used \(\epsilon = 10\) (vs. PPO's typical 0.2), KL coefficient 0.001, \(G = 16\) responses per prompt, max length 32,768 tokens.

Dr. GRPO (2025) fixed a length bias in GRPO where longer incorrect responses received disproportionate gradient signal. The fix is simple — normalize per-token rather than per-sequence — and achieves state-of-the-art results in just 27 hours on 8×A100 GPUs.

Step 7

RLVR: From Human Judgment to Verifiable Ground Truth

The most consequential shift in RL for LLMs is the move from human preference labels to verifiable rewards. Traditional RLHF asks humans "which response do you prefer?" This is expensive, noisy, subjective, and hard to scale. RLVR asks: "is the answer correct?"

For math, you check the final answer against ground truth. For code, you run unit tests. For logic puzzles, you verify the solution. These are rule-based reward functions — cheap, deterministic, infinitely scalable. DeepSeek R1 used only two reward signals: an accuracy reward (is the final answer correct?) and a format reward (did the model use the right \(\langle\text{think}\rangle\) /\(\langle\text{answer}\rangle\) structure?).

This is a fundamentally different kind of reward model design (Step 3). Instead of training a neural network to predict human preferences, you write a program — a verifier — that checks correctness deterministically. The environment (Step 2) consists of problems with known answers. The reward is binary: 1 if correct, 0 if not.

This decouples scaling from human labor. You can do millions of RL rollouts without paying a single annotator. The limiting factor becomes compute, not human bandwidth.

A fascinating open question from NeurIPS 2025 (Best Paper Runner-up): does RLVR actually teach the model new reasoning, or does it just surface reasoning abilities already latent from pretraining? The evidence suggests it's largely the latter — RLVR acts as a "self-distillation" that concentrates probability mass on correct reasoning paths. But even that is enormously valuable.

Compare RLHF (noisy human rewards) vs RLVR (precise verifiable rewards). As verifier accuracy approaches 1.0, RLVR converges faster with less variance.

The reward hacking problem — even with verifiers

Even verifiable rewards aren't immune to gaming. A model might output "The answer is 42" without reasoning (correct but useless), or find formatting tricks that game the verifier. The DeepSeek R1 paper found that process reward models (PRMs), which score intermediate reasoning steps, didn't help much compared to outcome-based rewards at scale. However, more recent work (DeepSeekMath-V2, 2025) suggests "explanation scoring" may become more important as models strengthen.

Interlude

RLAIF: When the AI Grades Itself

Between RLHF and RLVR sits another important paradigm: Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF), pioneered by Anthropic's Constitutional AI (2022). The idea: instead of paying humans to rank model outputs, use another LLM to provide the preference signal, guided by a written set of principles (the "constitution").

The training loop becomes self-referential: the model generates a response, a stronger (or same) model critiques it according to the constitution ("Is this response helpful? Is it harmful?"), and the critique derives preference labels. These labels train a reward model (Step 3), which feeds into PPO (Step 4) — just like RLHF, but with no human in the loop for the preference labeling step.

RLAIF scales alignment without human bottlenecks, while handling the subjective, open-ended tasks (tone, safety, nuance) where RLVR's rule-based verifiers don't apply. Google's 2023 study showed RLAIF can match RLHF quality on summarization and dialogue tasks.

A note on "RLAIVR": this isn't an established acronym. You may be thinking of a combination of RLAIF (AI feedback for subjective tasks) and RLVR (verifiable rewards for objective tasks). In practice, frontier labs use both simultaneously — RLVR for math/code/reasoning where ground truth exists, and RLAIF for helpfulness, safety, and style where it doesn't. The two are complementary, and modern pipelines blend them across different RL stages.

Step 8

Scaling RL Training: The Engineering Challenge

RL training at scale is fundamentally harder to engineer than pretraining. In pretraining, you have one model doing one thing (forward + backward pass on data). RL training requires orchestrating multiple interleaved workloads: generating rollouts (inference), computing rewards, and updating the policy (training) — plus a reference model for KL computation, and optionally a reward model or critic.

The key bottleneck is rollout generation. Recall from Step 2 that each RL "episode" is a complete response generation — autoregressive, token by token. For each training step, the policy must generate many complete responses (e.g., DeepSeek sampled 16 per prompt, with up to 32K tokens each). This is inherently sequential and memory-bound.

Modern frameworks attack this with several strategies:

Colocated model offloading — the policy model serves double duty for both training and generation. During rollout, training weights are offloaded to CPU; during training, inference state is freed. Meta's LlamaRL (2025) achieved a 10.7× speedup on 405B-parameter models using this approach.

Asynchronous off-policy training — don't wait for rollouts to finish before starting the next gradient update. Use slightly stale rollout data (off-policy) and compensate with importance sampling corrections. This keeps GPUs busy instead of idle.

Disaggregated infrastructure — separate the GPU clusters for inference and training, connected by high-speed networking. Each can be independently scaled and optimized.

Selective rollout (GRESO, 2025) — don't generate rollouts for prompts where the model already gets the answer right consistently. Focus compute on the "learning frontier" where the model is uncertain. This achieves 2.4× speedup in rollout time.

Sync training (0% overlap) wastes GPUs during rollout. Async training overlaps inference and gradient computation for near-linear scaling.

Putting It Together

The story of RL for LLMs is a story of progressive simplification and scaling — in both algorithms and infrastructure. Environment construction (Step 2) maps language generation to an MDP with enormous action spaces and sparse rewards. Reward models (Step 3) provide the compass, but their proxy nature means Goodhart's Law is always lurking.

RLHF with PPO (Step 4) proved the concept but was expensive — four models in memory, human annotators in the loop. DPO (Step 5) simplified the algorithm to a single loss function but sacrificed exploration. GRPO (Step 6) kept online RL's exploration while cutting the critic model, halving memory requirements. RLVR (Step 7) replaced human rewards with verifiable ones — decoupling scaling from human labor. RLAIF handles the subjective tasks where verifiers don't apply.

On the engineering side (Step 8), async training, disaggregated inference, and selective rollout are making RL at 400B+ parameter scale practical. The direction is clear: RL is no longer a niche post-training step. It is becoming the primary driver of capability gains in frontier LLMs.
